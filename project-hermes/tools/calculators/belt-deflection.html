▶ How to Measure Belt Deflection
What You Need
  1. A straight edge (ruler, level, or string) long enough to span between pulleys
  2. A spring scale (belt tension gauge) or a known weight — measures force in pounds
  3. A ruler or tape to measure deflection distance
A dedicated belt tension gauge (Browning, Gates, or Krikit) combines the force and distance measurement in one tool. Under $20 and worth having in your bag.
Step 1 — Find the Span Length
  1. Measure from the point where the belt leaves one pulley to the point where it contacts the other pulley
  2. This is the free span — the unsupported belt length between sheaves
  3. If you can't measure directly: lay a straight edge across the tops of both pulleys and measure the distance between contact points
Do NOT use center-to-center distance. Span length is always shorter than center distance because the belt wraps around each pulley.
Step 2 — Measure Deflection
  1. Place a straight edge across the tops of both pulleys, resting on the belt at each contact point
  2. At the midpoint of the span, press down on the belt with your spring scale perpendicular to the straight edge
  3. Push until the belt deflects 1/64" per inch of span length (the target deflection)
  4. Read the force on the spring scale at that deflection
Example: 32" span → target deflection = 32/64 = ½". Push the belt down ½" and read the force.
Step 3 — Compare to Spec
The force you measured should fall within the range for your belt section. If it doesn't:
  1. Force too low → belt is too loose → tighten by increasing center distance
  2. Force too high → belt is too tight → loosen by decreasing center distance
New belts should be tensioned to the higher end of the range. They will stretch and settle into the run-in range within 24–48 hours of operation. Re-check tension after the first day of run time.
Quick Field Method (No Gauge)
If you don't have a spring scale:
  1. Calculate the target deflection (span ÷ 64)
  2. Press the belt at mid-span with your thumb
  3. With moderate thumb pressure (roughly 5–8 lbs), an A/4L belt should deflect close to the target distance
  4. If you can easily push it past the target → too loose. If you can barely move it → too tight
The thumb method is an estimate only. Use a tension gauge for critical applications, multi-belt drives, and any drive over 5 HP.
